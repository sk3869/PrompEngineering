Brutalism

1950s–1970s

Brutalism emerged in postwar architecture as a response to social need and material honesty. Designers such as Alison and Peter Smithson emphasized exposed concrete, structural clarity, and an unpolished aesthetic that rejected decorative surfaces. The movement valued transparency of construction, revealing beams, joints, and textures as part of the visual language. In graphic design, Brutalism reappears as stark layouts, monospaced type, and raw HTML‑like interfaces that foreground structure over polish. Its influence persists in contemporary web design, where stripped‑down systems highlight hierarchy, function, and the underlying grid rather than stylistic embellishment.

material honesty structural clarity raw layout modular grid functionalism

Examples: Victoria & Albert Museum — Brutalist Architecture Collection · Canadian Centre for Architecture — Brutalism Archive

Not to be confused with Minimalism, because Brutalism foregrounds material roughness while Minimalism emphasizes smooth reduction.

Kinetic Typography

20th Century–Present

Kinetic typography refers to the use of animated or motion‑driven text to convey meaning, rhythm, and emphasis. Emerging from early film title design and later expanded through digital tools, it treats typography as a temporal medium rather than a static form. Designers manipulate scale, pacing, rotation, and spatial transitions to guide attention and reinforce narrative tone. Kinetic typography appears in film credits, advertising, motion graphics, and interactive interfaces, where movement clarifies hierarchy and emotional intent. Its principles parallel spoken language, using timing and gesture to shape how viewers interpret information across time.

motion graphics temporal hierarchy typographic rhythm spatial transition expressive movement

Examples: Cooper Hewitt — Digital Typography Collection · Letterform Archive — Motion Graphics Holdings

Not to be confused with New Wave Typography, because kinetic typography relies on motion over time, while New Wave experiments with static spatial disruption.

Gestalt Principles

1910s–Present

Gestalt principles originate from early 20th‑century psychology, proposing that humans perceive visual information as organized patterns rather than isolated elements. Concepts such as proximity, similarity, closure, continuity, and figure–ground explain how viewers group shapes, interpret hierarchy, and resolve incomplete forms. Designers use these principles to structure layouts, guide attention, and create intuitive relationships between text and imagery. Gestalt theory underpins modern interface design, branding systems, and typographic composition, ensuring that complex information feels coherent. Its influence extends across disciplines because it describes universal perceptual tendencies rather than stylistic preferences.

proximity similarity figure–ground continuity closure

Examples: Bauhaus‑Archiv Museum — Gestalt Teaching Materials · MoMA — Graphic Design and Perception Collection

Not to be confused with optical illusions, because Gestalt principles describe perception rules, not tricks or distortions.
